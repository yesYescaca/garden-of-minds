****GARDEN OF MINDS****

***ENTRY 001 - WHY DOES INTELLIGENCE EXIST?***

Today I begin what i am calling the Garden of Minds.
I don't know exactly what this project will become next year, or in five or ten, twenty, or fifty years. For now it is an attempt for me to understand and document intelligence, minds, intelligent life and the many possible ways they may exist in the universe.

The question that motivated me to start this project for now is:
How would I recognize intelligence if it looked nothing like a human?

We humans often imagine intelligent life as something similar to ourselves. We often imagine aliens having spaceships, advanced civilizations, technology, science, language, cities and culture. However, I think this way of thinking of intelligent life is very human-centered. And I get it, we humans are only one species among millions on Earth, and have become the most abundant and successful in all parts of the planet. We also tend to think, imagine and associate things in a very anthropogenic or human-like way.

But who is to say that human intelligence is the template for all intelligence?

This made me realise that in reality, looking at other intelligent animals like crows, dolphins and chimpanzees, human intelligence may not be intelligence itself, but one example of intelligence. 

To explore this idea, I started with a basic question: Why are we humans intelligent at all?

My first thoughts, as a biology student, were in fact biological. Humans evolved from primate ancestors living in changing and complex environments filled with information, dangers, opportunities, and social interactions. Primates already possessed many traits that may have encouraged intelligence, such as flexible limbs capable of manipulating objects and highly social lifestyles communicating and cooperating together between individuals.

When early humans moved into more open environments in the savannahs of Africa, they became more exposed to predators and environmental changes. Unlike many animals, humans were not highly speciallized physically since we were not particularly fast, strong, armored, or dangerous on our own. Instead, we relied increasingly on our brains.

At first, I thought environmental complexity might explain the evolution of intelligence, but this explanation quickly seemed incomplete. Many species live in complex environments and process enourmous amounts of information, yet ionly one lineage produced us humans. This therefore suggests that something else was important in our evolution of intelligence.

I began to suspect that social life may have played a larger role than i originally thought, as living in groups creates difficult problems. You never fully know who can be trusted, who is lying, who is allied to whom, who owns favours, who is likely to help or betray, and these questions require understanding the behaviour and intentions of other minds. 

Perhaprs human intelligence was originally social intelligence, or perhaps many of the abilities we now use for mathematics, science, engineering, and philosophy evolved because our ancestors needed to navigate increasingly complex social worlds.

This then led me to compare humans and ants since both display similar behaviours, and some of these present in ants we tend to often associate with intelligence. Ants cooperate, communicate, wage wars, build structures, farm fungi, and organize labour. Yet most people would not consider an ant colony to be intelligent in the sam way a human society is, and at first, I thought this difference might be explained by brain size. Indivual ants possess extremely small brains and therefore limited individual cognitive abilities.z

However, I relaised that maybe I was asking the wrong question. Sure, an individual ant is "less intelligent" than humans, but perhaps I should ask whether they are intelligent in a completely different way. Humans seem to concentrate intelligence individualistically, whereas ants seem to distribute intelligence acrosss the colony.

A human scientist may carry knowledge that exist nowhere else. An individual ant carries very little knowledge, but the colony as a whole can sole complex problems. This lead me to another realisation: that intelligence may not belong solely to individuals, but also to groups, colonies, ecosystems, civilizations or other systems that process information collectively. 

The more I thought about this, the less I thought of intelligence as a single scale, since we humans often imagine and interpret intelligence as a ladder, classifying us at the top of it and "less intelligent" species below us like chimps, dogs, mouses, and ants. However, intelligent may not be a ladder at all but a landscape.

- Some species specialize in social intelligence like us humans
- Some species specialize in collective intelligence like ants
- Some species specialize in spatial intelligence
- Or maybe specialize in prediction or communication

Instead of being more or less intelligent, they may simply be solving different problems in order to survive or keep existing. Survival is not just mainly about who is more adapted or less adapted: it is about solving different problems in an environment in order to be able to survive and reproduce; to continue your existence. 

This idea became more interesting when I began thinking about artifficial intelligence. Current AI models and systems like ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude and others are not alive, yet many can perform tasks that clearlt require sophisticated information processing. This therefore forced me to reconsider another assumption: perhaps intelligence does not require life but something else.

One possibility is that intelligence emerges whenever a system can process information, build models of the world, pursue goals, and adapt its behaviour. However, this definition remains incomplete, as ideally a thermostat receives information from its environment and reacts to it, yet I would not call it intelligent even though it fits this definition. Why?

My answer to this is that a thermostat possesses no goal of its own. It is not attempting to survive, persist, or achieve anything beyond following a simple control rule. 

Whether this distinction survives future investigation remains uncertain.

Another important idea emerged when considering hypothetical alien minds. Imagine an alien species with no language, no tools, no technology, and no mathematics, and suppose it could nevertheless predict future events with incredible accuracy and maintain detailed internal models of its environment. Would such species be intelligent?

I believe the answer is yes.

This realization may be one of the most important conclusions i made from this discussion. Intelligence should not be defined by human abilities, but by what a system can do. 

As a results, I am becoming increasingly suspicious of statements such as "Humans are the only intelligent species we know of" or "Intelligent aliens would probably look technologically advanced". Both statements may reveal more about human assumptions than about intelligence itself.

The Garden of Minds therefore does not begin with an answer, but a question:

*If intelligence can exist in many forms, how can we learn to recognize it?*


I suspect this question will guide much of this journey ahead.
